\section{Introduction}

\subsection{What is a Forward Curve?}

When a commodity is traded on a futures exchange, it is not only the current
price that is visible --- dozens of contracts with different delivery dates
are quoted simultaneously. The set of all these prices, plotted against their
maturity, forms what is called a \textbf{forward curve}.

The shape of this curve is not random. It directly reflects the market's
collective view of supply, demand, and storage conditions over the coming
months. Two fundamental structures exist:

\begin{keybox}{Key definitions}
\textbf{Backwardation}: near-term prices higher than deferred prices. The market is paying a premium for immediate delivery, signalling tight supply or low inventories.\\[4pt]
\textbf{Contango}: deferred prices higher than near-term prices. Storage costs and a comfortable supply balance push future prices above today's spot.
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Why Does It Matter?}

Reading the forward curve correctly allows traders, risk managers and industrial buyers to answer practical questions every day:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=4pt]
\item Is the market tight right now, or are inventories building?
\item Should I lock in a fixed price today, or wait for deferred contracts?
\item Does the market expect the current price level to persist, or to mean-revert?
\item What is the implied cost of rolling a long futures position forward each month?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{What is CFCAP?}

\textbf{CFCAP} (Commodity Forward Curve Analytics Platform) is a Python application
that automates the full analysis pipeline: it downloads live futures prices, fits a
parametric model to the curve, computes financial metrics, generates trading signals,
and presents all results in an interactive dashboard.

It covers more than \textbf{65 commodities} across 8 asset classes, from WTI Crude
Oil to LME Nickel, EU Carbon allowances and Baltic freight rates.

\section{Forward Curve Analytics}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{The Nelson-Siegel Model}

The Nelson-Siegel model (1987) was originally developed for fixed-income yield
curves. Applied to commodity futures, it fits a smooth parametric function to
the observed prices and extracts four parameters with direct economic meaning.

\begin{equation}
F(T) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \varphi(T,\tau)
     + \beta_2 \cdot \bigl[\varphi(T,\tau) - e^{-T/\tau}\bigr],
\quad
\varphi(T,\tau) = \frac{1 - e^{-T/\tau}}{T/\tau}
\end{equation}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{c >{\bfseries}l X l}
\toprule
Param. & Name & What it tells us & Example (WTI) \\
\midrule
$\beta_0$ & Long-term level  & The price the curve converges to as maturity grows --- the model's estimate of long-run fair value. If spot is well above $\beta_0$, the market expects prices to fall. & 68 \$/bbl \\
$\beta_1$ & Slope            & The difference between the current spot and the long-term level. A large negative $\beta_1$ means strong backwardation --- the front of the curve is much higher than the back. & $-8$ \$/bbl \\
$\beta_2$ & Curvature        & A hump or valley in the middle of the curve. This arises during seasonal transitions (e.g.\ winter gas demand) or when a short-term shock affects only part of the maturity spectrum. & $+2$ \$/bbl \\
$\tau$    & Decay speed      & How quickly the curve converges to $\beta_0$. A small $\tau$ means the shock is short-lived; a large $\tau$ means the market expects conditions to persist for many months. & 6 months \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{center}

\begin{notebox}
\textbf{Robust fitting:} A naive fit often produces degenerate results where one
parameter diverges to an extreme value (e.g.\ $\beta_0 = 3671$ for Palladium
at \$1\,100/oz). CFCAP uses a three-attempt strategy: (1) full NS with $\beta_2$
bounded to $\pm8\%$ of spot; (2) NS with $\beta_2 = 0$ for monotone curves;
(3) exponential fallback $F(T) = F_\infty + (S - F_\infty)\,e^{-kT}$ for
sparse curves with fewer than 5 contracts.
\end{notebox}

\subsection{Implied Convenience Yield}

The convenience yield is one of the most important quantities in commodity
finance. It measures the implied benefit of holding the \textit{physical} commodity
rather than a futures contract --- the insurance value of immediate availability.

It is derived by inverting the cost-of-carry equation:

\begin{equation}
F(T) = S \cdot e^{(r + u - cy)\,T}
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
cy(T) = r + u - \frac{1}{T}\ln\!\left(\frac{F(T)}{S}\right)
\end{equation}

where $r$ is the risk-free rate, $u$ the annualised storage cost, and $S$ the spot price.

\medskip

\begin{center}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{>{\centering\arraybackslash}l >{\centering\arraybackslash}l X}
\toprule
Condition & Signal & Economic interpretation \\
\midrule
$cy > 2 \times r$ & \textcolor{signalgreen}{\textbf{BUY}} & The market is paying a large premium for physical ownership. Inventories are likely low, supply is tight, and holders of the physical commodity earn a substantial implied yield. \\
$r < cy < 2 \times r$ & Neutral & Positive carry, but not strong enough to drive aggressive accumulation. \\
$0 < cy < r$ & Neutral & Positive but below the cost of financing. Physical storage is barely economic. \\
$cy < 0$ & \textcolor{signalred}{\textbf{SELL}} & The forward premium exceeds all carry costs. A classic cash-and-carry arbitrage: buy spot, store, sell forward at a profit. Signals a surplus market. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{center}

\subsection{Roll Yield}

When an investor holds a long futures position, the front-month contract expires
every month and must be \textit{rolled} into the next one. The cost or gain of
this operation is the roll yield:

\begin{equation}
ry(T) = \frac{S - F(T)}{F(T) \cdot T}
\end{equation}

In backwardation ($S > F(T)$), the roll yield is \textbf{positive}: rolling from
M1 to M2 generates a gain because M2 is cheaper than M1, and it will
eventually converge up to the spot price. This rewards passive long investors.

In contango ($S < F(T)$), the roll yield is \textbf{negative}: each monthly
roll costs money because the new contract is more expensive. This drag can
amount to 10--15\% per year and explains why many commodity ETFs significantly
underperform the spot price over time.

\subsection{Calendar Spreads}

A calendar spread is the price difference between two futures contracts:

\[
\text{Spread}_{i \to j} = F(T_j) - F(T_i), \quad T_j > T_i
\]

CFCAP computes all consecutive M+1 spreads along the curve. A negative spread
means the near contract is more expensive (backwardation); a positive spread
means the deferred contract is more expensive (contango).

The platform counts how many months are in each regime and highlights the
most significant spread legs for potential calendar spread trades.

\section{Trading Signal Engine}

\subsection{Overview}

The signal engine is one of the most distinctive features of CFCAP. Rather than
simply displaying numbers, it evaluates \textbf{51 rule-based conditions}
against the live analytics and produces actionable outputs with a plain-language
rationale for each one.

Every signal card in the dashboard shows:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
\item The \textbf{signal level}: BUY, SELL, SPREAD, HEDGE, ARBITRAGE, WARNING, or NEUTRAL
\item The \textbf{trigger values}: the exact numbers that activated the rule (e.g.\ \texttt{CY +14.2\% >> 2.5x RF (12.5\%)})
\item The \textbf{rationale}: a short explanation of why the condition is significant and what it implies for positioning
\end{itemize}

\section{Historical Comparison and Persistence}
% =============================================================================

A single forward curve snapshot is informative, but the real insight comes from
comparison over time. CFCAP saves a CSV snapshot of every curve it downloads,
organised by commodity and date.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=bash]
data/
|-- curves/wti_crude_oil/
|   |-- 2026-04-07.csv    # today
|   |-- 2026-03-31.csv    # 7 days ago
|   |-- 2026-03-24.csv    # 14 days ago
|   |-- ...
|-- dashboards/wti_crude_oil/
|   |-- wti_crude_oil_20260407_091522.png
|-- eia_cache/            # 24h TTL
|-- logs/scheduler.log
\end{lstlisting}

After two weeks of daily scheduler runs, the J-7 and J-14 comparisons on the
Forward Curve chart use \textbf{real historical data} rather than simulated
shifts. This allows the user to see exactly how the curve has moved and
whether the move was a parallel shift (macro driver) or a twist (supply shock
concentrated at the front end).

The Streamlit dashboard includes a \textbf{date selector} in the Forward Curve
tab. The user can overlay the current curve with any saved snapshot to
visualise the exact curve shape before and after any market event.

\section{Possible Improvements}
% =============================================================================

The platform is already functional across 65 commodities. Several directions
could extend its analytical depth and usability.

\subsection{Quantitative Extensions}

\textbf{Nelson-Siegel-Svensson model.}
The NSS model adds a second curvature term, making it better suited to
longer-dated curves with more complex shapes such as TTF Natural Gas at 24
months:
\[
F(T) = \beta_0 + \beta_1\,\varphi(T,\tau_1) + \beta_2\bigl[\varphi(T,\tau_1) - e^{-T/\tau_1}\bigr] + \beta_3\bigl[\varphi(T,\tau_2) - e^{-T/\tau_2}\bigr]
\]

\textbf{Asian option pricer.}
Many commodity hedges use options on the \textit{average} price over a period
rather than a fixed-date option. Using the NS term structure as the diffusion
input and a Monte Carlo simulation, CFCAP could estimate the fair value of
Asian calls and puts --- directly applicable to jet fuel and crude oil
hedging programmes.

\textbf{Historical realised volatility.}
Once a sufficient number of daily snapshots have been saved, rolling 20-day
and 60-day realised volatility can be computed from the daily spot returns.
A volatility cone overlaid on the forward curve would help option traders
assess whether implied volatility is rich or cheap.

\textbf{Portfolio VaR.}
By defining a book of futures positions (volume, maturity, direction) and
applying Monte Carlo curve shocks, the platform could compute Value-at-Risk
at 95\% and 99\% confidence for a realistic commodity derivatives portfolio.

\subsection{Data and Infrastructure}

\textbf{SQLite database.}
Replacing the CSV files with a local SQLite database would allow faster
historical queries, rolling cross-commodity correlation analysis, and
more complex multi-date comparisons.

\textbf{FastAPI REST endpoint.}
Exposing the analytics as JSON at \texttt{/curve/\{commodity\}} and
\texttt{/signals/\{commodity\}} would make the platform consumable by
Excel via Power Query, Power BI dashboards, or other automated scripts.

\textbf{Configuration file.}
Moving API keys, the scheduler time and the default batch list to a
\texttt{config.yaml} file (excluded from version control) would follow
security best practices and make the platform easier to deploy.

\subsection{User Experience}

\textbf{Morning report.}
An automated HTML report generated after each scheduler run, containing
all charts and signal summaries, could be sent by email every morning
before the trading day begins.

\textbf{Real-time alerts.}
Email or Telegram notifications triggered by specific conditions ---
such as a convenience yield crossing $2 \times r$ or a regime shift
from contango to backwardation --- would add a real-time monitoring layer.

\textbf{Multi-commodity overview.}
A landing page in Streamlit showing one row per commodity, with spot,
structure, roll yield and dominant signal, would allow a trader to
monitor an entire portfolio at a glance.

\section{Conclusion}
% =============================================================================

CFCAP demonstrates how a complete, professional-grade quantitative analytics
platform can be built with Python and freely available market data.

By combining live data acquisition from two sources, Nelson-Siegel curve fitting
with a robust multi-attempt strategy, cost-of-carry analytics including
convenience yield and roll yield, a 51-signal trading engine grounded in
financial theory, and an interactive browser dashboard, the platform provides
a rigorous and practical tool for anyone working with commodity derivatives.

Its single-file architecture makes it easy to deploy and extend: new commodities
can be added to the registry in a few lines, new signals can be plugged into the
engine without touching the rest of the code, and the modular design makes it
straightforward to integrate more advanced models as the project evolves.
